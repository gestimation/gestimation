From Coffee Chat to Research − Study design I

　　　　　　　　　　　　Keywords: OS/PFS/DFS/response, PICO/PECO, observational study

A daughter starting her first research, and her statistician father

Me: “So… you really are a professor in statistics, right?”

Dad: “In Japan, yes. One of the many.”

Me: “My boss keeps telling me it’s time I do ‘some research’ and present at a conference. He’s really into supporting cancer patients in returning to work since the Basic Plan to Promote Cancer Control Programs in Japan was revised. Long story short, I think he wants me to run some kind of survey. I’m not against it, but this is clearly your territory, isn’t it? Statistics and all that.”

Dad: “Half my territory, half yours. You see the patients, I only see the data. So, do you have any clinical question or hypothesis in mind?”

Me: “Honestly? Not really. I was sort of hoping I could just collect a lot of data first and think later. Is that illegal?”

Dad: “Not illegal, but not safe. Without at least a working hypothesis, it’s hard to design a good study.”

Me: “Okay, okay. If I have to say something… I’m curious about which patients have a harder time staying in work. Not just men vs women, but things like tumor stage and treatment. I work in GI surgery, so I keep wondering about patients with a stoma. Can they really keep doing the same job as before surgery?”

Dad: “That’s already a good start. You’re close to a research question.”

Me: “Really? I was just thinking out loud. Fine, I guess I’ll start drafting a questionnaire. That actually helped. Thanks.”

Dad: “Hold on. Have you decided on your study population yet?”

Me: “Um… patients with cancer who had surgery at our hospital?”

Dad: “Do you see many stomas in gastric cancer patients?”

Me: “It’s not impossible, but honestly, almost never.”

Dad: “Right. If your main interest is stomas, it might make sense to focus on cancers where stomas are common, like rectal cancer.”

Me: “So you’re saying narrow the population?”

Dad: “Exactly. If you narrow the target population, you can ask more detailed questions and improve comparability between groups. If you want to compare patients with and without a stoma, it’s better to keep the cancer site consistent.”

Me: “Comparability…such a statistics word. But yeah, if the background is all over the place, it’s a mess. I do get that.”

Dad: “On the other hand, if you broaden the population, you gain generalizability. If your goal were to estimate the return-to-work rate among all cancer survivors in Japan, you wouldn’t limit the cancer site, and you might want data from multiple hospitals, not just ours.”

Me: “Right now, with only our hospital, calling it a ‘real-world survey’ would be a bit bold. So ‘high generalizability’ basically means ‘other hospitals could actually use the results as a reference’?”

Dad: “That’s a good way to put it. See, you do think statistically.”

Me: “Don’t flatter me just to get free clinical consulting.”

Dad: “In class, I tell my students this:

When your clinical question is still vague,
try to express it using PICO or PECO before you design the study.

It sounds fancy, but it’s just a framework.”

Me: “I’ve heard the words PICO, PECO…but nobody really explains what to do with them.”

Dad: “Let’s walk through it using your case. In PICO/PECO:

  • What kind of patients? (Patients/Population)
  • What factor are we focusing on? (Intervention/Exposure)
  • What are we comparing it to? (Comparison)
  • What outcomes are we interested in? (Outcome)

These four pieces are like the minimum ingredients for a study recipe. In a clinical trial, we usually talk about Intervention (I). In observational studies like your survey, we talk about Exposure (E).”

Me: “Okay, P is like, ‘rectal cancer patients after curative surgery in our hospital’. For E and C… maybe ‘with stoma’ vs ‘without stoma’?”

Dad: “Exactly. And O is your outcome, that is to say, what you want to measure. In your case, something about return to work.”

Me: “So outcome is the thing we call OS, DFS, response rate, that kind of stuff?”

Dad: “Right. In statistical analysis, the outcome variable is the most important part. So deciding what the outcome is is a key step in planning.”

Me: “Can I really fix that before collecting any data? Feels like I’m committing too early.”

Dad: “If you don’t fix it before designing the questionnaire, you’ll regret it later. Once you start collecting data, by the way, what software are you planning to use?”

Me: “R. That’s what everyone in my department uses.”

Dad: “How comfortable are you with R?”

Me: “We had it in undergrad, but at this point I’ve forgotten everything except that semicolons stress me out less than parentheses.”

Dad: “So we’ll need to get you used to R again. Which R functions you use will depend on the type of outcome you choose.”

Me: “So step zero is:

  1. Turn my vague hunch into a proper question using P, E, C, O
  2. Decide on the outcome
  3. Then figure out how to survive R and not embarrass myself at journal club

That’s the plan?”

Dad: “That’s a very good summary. If you bring me questions like that, I’m happy to be used as a resource.”

Me: “Deal. I’ll bring the clinical mess, you translate it into statistics. Very efficient division of labor.”

Clinical questions and research hypotheses

In everyday clinical work in Japan, you naturally start to notice small curiosities:

  • “Do my older patients really have worse outcomes?”
  • “Is this new support program actually helping?”

These are clinical questions. To turn them into research, we want to re-express them as research hypotheses. A research hypothesis is built from the minimum elements needed for study design:

  • P: Patients/Population
  • I/E: Intervention/Exposure
  • C: Comparison
  • O: Outcome

That’s exactly what PICO/PECO helps you write down.

Example: Research hypothesis 1

Among rectal cancer patients after curative resection in Japan, is there a difference in return-to-work within one year between those with and without a stoma?

  • P: Rectal cancer patients after curative resection
  • E: Stoma present
  • C: Stoma absent
  • O: Returned to work within one year after surgery (yes/no)

Here you’d compare the return-to-work proportion between the stoma and non-stoma groups.

What to choose for C (Comparison) is important. If you only know the return-to-work rate in the stoma group, it’s hard to say whether that number is “high” or “low”.
Having a clear comparison group improves interpretability.

Example: Research hypothesis 2

Among cancer patients after curative surgery, does providing a guidebook on balancing work and cancer treatment increase the proportion who return to work within one year?

  • P: Patients with cancer after curative surgery
  • E: Received the guidebook
  • C: Did not receive the guidebook
  • O: Returned to work within one year after surgery (yes/no)

Here P is broader, so the results could potentially apply to a wider range of patients. This is an example of higher generalizability..

Example: Research hypothesis 3 (exploratory)

“Are sex, age, stage, adjuvant chemotherapy, performance status, comorbidities, stoma, preoperative employment status, household income, cancer insurance, and use of employment support services associated with return-to-work within one year?”

In exploratory studies like this, P is clear, but E and C are not just one thing — we’re screening multiple potential risk factors.

So “structuring the question” doesn’t mean you have to force every study into a clean PECO shape. What matters is to identify the minimal set of elements needed to design a coherent study for your question.

A quiz related to this episode

When formulating study design, the Latin expression “ceteris paribus” often appears. Which of the following best describes its meaning?

  1. If there is a causal relationship, it will inevitably occur
  2. A universally generalizable law
  3. All other conditions being equal
  4. Observing a sufficiently large amount of data
Answer
  • The correct answer is 3.

The concept of “ceteris paribus” is almost equal to the conditions of compatibility.
